I am currently a first year student at UC Riverside. I am currently Undeclared but I plan on switching into a psychology major before my second year. I want to study behavioral patterns in youth and I am open to all fields of study within and outside of psychology. I am also drawn to environmental conservation issues and I would love to join a project to help with this issue. In my own life, I am very aware of my carbon footprint and where my money goes. I do this through second hand shopping, supporting smaller businesses as well as carpooling or using public transport. Through my time here at UCR, the classes I have taken have allowed me to be more environmentally conscious and helped me alter the way I live and think about different issues, such as food waste. My thinking has slowed down, allowing me to be even more conscious of the daily choices I make, choices I would have made without a second though, even a few months ago.

Living on campus has also allowed me to be more empathetic towards others and better understand different mental illnesses and see how my peers are treated and how their experiences are with getting treatment. This also allows me to see the importance of our student health services office and how big of a role many of the therapists and psychiatrists play in students' lives. This inspires and draws me more to the field of psychology, seeing how much of a difference one conversion can make in a student's life. My end goal for my profession is to be somewhere where I am able to actively make a difference within someone else's life for the better, and help future generations.

Another issue I am interested in studying is the role music in any form affects us. In my own life music has been a very big factor in determining how I am feeling, and I would be very interested in seeing if other individuals experience the same issues and if music has the ability to change one's mood, even if there is no emotional connection to the song itself. In my own life, I have experimented with classical conditioning with my cat rosie. I would train her with either a clap or a bell, and train her to associate that noise with either a treat or her favorite wet food. Even after I stopped the experiment, she would still associate the clap or bell with food and meow for it. I also tried the same technique with my friend's dog, and now this helps her keep control of her dog on walks and around other dogs.
